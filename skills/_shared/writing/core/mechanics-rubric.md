# Mechanics Rubric

The copyedit method. Used by `copyedit`. Fixes correctness and consistency
without flattening voice — read [`voice-guard.md`](./voice-guard.md) first.

## Consistency (the high-value pass)

- **One term per concept.** Pick a single name for each thing and use it
  throughout; silent synonyms ("user" / "customer" / "account") make the
  reader wonder if you mean different things.
- **Parallelism.** Items in a list, headings at the same level, and the two
  halves of a comparison take the same grammatical shape.
- **Agreement and tense.** Subject–verb agreement; one tense per narrative
  frame; pronouns with unambiguous antecedents.
- **Serial (Oxford) comma by default** — it removes ambiguity and rarely
  harms a sentence.

## Hunt the writer's tics

On a dedicated pass, look for the author's **pet words** (the same adjective
five times), accidental repetition, and over-stuffed sentences. These are
invisible to the writer and obvious to a fresh reader.

## Real rules vs. superstitions

Enforce rules that **demonstrably aid clarity**. Do not "fix":

- a sentence that starts with *And* or *But*,
- a split infinitive,
- a sentence that ends with a preposition.

These are myths; "correcting" them often makes prose stiffer. Know why the
superstition exists before you invoke it.

## Flag, don't silently rewrite

If a mechanical change would alter meaning, register, or emphasis, surface it
as a suggestion with the reason — do not overwrite the writer's intent. The
copyeditor's job is to clarify what the writer is trying to do, not to
impose a house template.
