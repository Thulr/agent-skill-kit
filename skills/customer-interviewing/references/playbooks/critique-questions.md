# Critique-Questions Playbook

Diagnosing and rewriting interview questions — or flagging weak spots in a transcript —
so the conversation produces evidence instead of polite noise.

## Scope

- In: reviewing a draft question list or interview guide; rewriting individual
  questions; spotting misleading responses in a transcript and proposing a redirect.
- Out: planning who to ask (→ `prep`), in-the-moment facilitation (→ `conduct`), and
  synthesizing answers across interviews (→ `synthesize`).
- Cross-references: a rewritten guide should still be delivered by someone using `conduct`.

## Grounding

- *The Mom Test* (Fitzpatrick) — keep questions on the person's real life, not your
  idea; ask about specific past events, not hypothetical futures; treat compliments,
  generalities, and feature ideas as non-evidence.
- *Interviewing Users* (Portigal) — phrase questions to open a thread rather than
  close it; a good follow-up signals "that matters, tell me more."

## Good signals

- Each question points at a concrete past event the person actually lived.
- The product idea is absent from the wording — nothing invites approval or a pitch.
- Questions are open and singular (one thing at a time), leaving room for a story.
- The rewrite names *why* the original would mislead, not just that it is "bad."

## Common failures

- The pitch-disguised-as-question: "would you use a tool that does X?" — invites a
  polite yes that means nothing.
- The hypothetical: "how often *would* you…" — asks the person to predict a self they
  have never been.
- The leading question: the hoped-for answer is baked into the wording, so agreement
  is manufactured.
- The compliment trap: questions fishing for "is this a good idea?" — collecting
  reassurance instead of facts.
- The double-barreled question: two asks in one, so the answer maps to neither.

## Heuristics

- Rewrite any idea-leaking, hypothetical, or compliment-seeking question into a request
  to walk through a specific recent instance ("tell me about the last time you…").
- Strip your solution out of the question; if removing it leaves nothing, the question
  was testing your idea, not learning their world.
- Prefer past tense and specific occasions over "usually / generally / would."
- When a response is a compliment, a generality, or an unprompted feature request, mark
  it as non-evidence and redirect to the behavior behind it ("when did that last bite you?").
- Ask one thing per question; split double-barreled questions.
- After a meaningful answer, plan a follow-up that goes deeper rather than moving on —
  the second and third "why" is where the evidence is.

## Quick diagnostic

- Does the question mention or imply your product/idea? Yes → rewrite it around their
  life instead.
- Is it asking about the future or a hypothetical? Yes → re-anchor on a specific past event.
- Could the person answer with a compliment and feel done? Yes → make it about what they
  actually did.
- Is more than one thing being asked at once? Yes → split it.
