# Audience Frame

Name the reader before you write or judge. Used across every intent; the
source of the reader **lenses** the review dispatch spawns.

## Beat the expert blind spot

You cannot easily imagine not knowing what you know, so you under-explain and
drop undefined jargon. Counter it deliberately: define terms on first use,
add one concrete example, and reread cold (or have a cold reader try the
piece). Most "unclear writing" is this blind spot, not bad sentences.

## Write from the reader's aspiration

Frame the piece around what the reader is trying to do or become, not around
what the author accomplished. Before drafting, state: who is this for, what do
they already know, what do they need to decide or do after reading.

## Reader lenses (for review and dispatch)

When auditing or pressure-testing a piece, read it as each of these in turn —
they catch different failures:

- **The time-pressed scanner** — skims headings and first lines only. Does the
  point survive skimming? Is the recommendation findable in 10 seconds?
- **The skeptical decision-maker** — looks for the ask, the stakes, and the
  evidence. Is the claim self-testable? Is the "so what" explicit?
- **The first-time / novice reader** — hits undefined terms and unstated
  assumptions. Where does the expert blind spot bite?
- **The implementer** (for instructions) — must act from the text. Is every
  step actionable, ordered, and complete?

Pick the lenses that fit the genre: a memo needs the scanner and the
decision-maker; a how-to needs the novice and the implementer.
