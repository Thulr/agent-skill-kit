## Paraphrased Concepts

- **Storymapping** (Lichaw): map the intended experience of use as a story, plot point by plot
  point, the way a screenwriter structures a film — to design experiences optimized for engagement.
- **User as protagonist**: the user (not the product) is the main character/hero; the product is
  what helps them reach their goal. Design from their want, not your features.
- **Three story types**: a **concept story** (the high-level value/why — what the product makes
  possible), an **origin story** (how a user discovers, arrives, and onboards), and a **usage
  story** (the core interaction loop in regular use).
- **Narrative arc**: exposition (the protagonist's starting situation) → inciting incident (the
  event/problem that creates a want) → rising action → crisis (the make-or-break moment) → climax
  (goal achieved or not) → falling action → resolution/end. The arc peaks near the end.
- **Map flows onto the arc**: plotting an experience on the arc surfaces how users emotionally move
  through it and where the story stalls, gaps, or anticlimaxes.
- **Story as a design and test tool**: use the arc to ideate, design, align a team, and test
  experiences — from a landing page to a product strategy.

## Reusable Behaviors

- Behavior: **Map an experience as a story arc** — cast the user-protagonist and their goal, then
  lay the beats (exposition → inciting incident → rising action → climax → resolution) for the
  chosen story type. Derived from: Lichaw arc + story types.
- Behavior: **Diagnose a flat or broken experience via story** — find the missing inciting incident,
  unclear protagonist goal, anticlimax, or the gap/cliffhanger where users drop. Derived from: arc-mapping.
- Behavior: **Craft a concept or origin story** to align stakeholders or pitch the experience vision.
  Derived from: three story types.

## Critical / Dissenting Takes

- Steelman (hypothesis, no published critic found): narrative structure can impose a tidy, linear
  hero's-arc on product use that is actually nonlinear, habitual, or multi-entry; not every
  interaction is a dramatic journey. Over-dramatizing mundane flows can mislead. Use story where the
  experience genuinely has an arc; for evaluating an existing interface's usability, a heuristic
  audit (`ux-audit`) is the better instrument.
