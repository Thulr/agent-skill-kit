# Trend / Horizon Playbook

## Scope

Where the world is moving. Whether the opportunity is too early, too
late, or in the window. Trend research separates signals (data) from
narratives (stories about data) and forces a timing call: now / wait
/ never / pivot.

- In: technology trends, customer behavior shifts, regulatory
  trends, competitive moves by incumbents and startups, macro
  trends (capital, labor, spending), platform shifts (search,
  cloud, AI), adjacent opportunities created by change, timing
  bets and timing risks.
- Out: market sizing today (`market.md`), specific competitor
  intelligence (`competitive.md`), specific regulatory exposure
  (`legal.md`), execution timing (operational planning is in
  `operational.md`).
- Intents this surface answers: scope, investigate, synthesize, decide.

## Grounding

- **Carlota Perez — *Technological Revolutions and Financial
  Capital* (2002)** — installation period vs deployment period;
  five surges; long-wave timing.
- **Clayton Christensen — *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997)** —
  disruption from below; incumbents' rational neglect of
  not-yet-profitable segments.
- **Andy Grove — *Only the Paranoid Survive* (1996)** — strategic
  inflection points; the moment a 10× change in some force
  reshapes the competitive landscape.
- **Convo.txt** — trend research covers 6+ shift categories and
  produces "a trend summary, strategic implications, things to
  monitor, timing bets — useful for deciding whether to move now,
  wait, or pivot."

## Good signals

- Signals are separated from narratives. A "trend" backed only by
  TechCrunch headlines is a narrative; backed by spend / hiring /
  search / regulation is signal.
- Each shift is named in 1 line: what's changing, evidence,
  estimated rate.
- The implication for *this* opportunity is named — not "AI is
  hot" but "AI hiring in target industry up 3× → buyer awareness
  shifts, our positioning needs to address it."
- Timing is called explicitly: too early / window open / too late
  / unclear. With named drivers for each.
- "Things to monitor" list is present — leading indicators we'd
  watch to reopen the timing call.
- Counter-trend signals are listed too (what would make this
  trend reverse or stall) — otherwise the playbook is a hype
  newsletter.
- Adjacent opportunities created by the trend are noted (without
  expanding scope away from the named bet).

## Common failures

- **Confusing narrative with signal.** "Everyone's talking about
  X" without evidence of spend, hiring, search, or regulatory
  change. Mitigation: every named trend cites a quantitative
  signal.
- **Trend without implication.** "AI is changing knowledge work."
  Therefore? Mitigation: every trend names the specific implication
  for THIS opportunity.
- **Timing-blind.** A real opportunity at the wrong time fails
  faster than a marginal opportunity at the right time. Mitigation:
  explicit too-early / window-open / too-late call.
- **Monoculture trend-watching.** Only watching tech trends in a
  business / regulatory / macro environment that's shifting too.
  Mitigation: scan ≥3 categories.
- **Trend-chasing collapsing the bet.** Pivoting at every new
  trend = no bet. Trend research informs the timing call; it
  doesn't redefine the bet weekly.
- **No counter-signals.** A risk register lists what could go
  wrong inside the bet; trend research should list what could
  reverse the tailwind. They're different.
- **Adjacent opportunities expanding scope.** "Since we're already
  doing X, we could also do Y." Note adjacents; don't pivot the
  scope mid-investigation.

## Heuristics

- **(scope, investigate)** *Signal vs narrative.* For each "trend":
  what's the quantitative signal (spend, hiring, search, regulatory
  change)? If only narrative, downgrade confidence to L and
  re-search for signal.
- **(investigate)** *Scan ≥3 categories.* Tech, customer behavior,
  regulatory, competitive, macro, platform. Monoculture watching
  misses the reshaping force.
- **(investigate, decide)** *Trend ⇒ implication ⇒ decision.* Each
  trend has a named implication for THIS opportunity, with a
  decision (positioning shift / new risk / new monitor / no
  change).
- **(investigate, decide)** *Timing call: too-early / window-open
  / too-late / unclear.* With named drivers. "Unclear" is fine as
  long as it names what would resolve it.
- **(investigate)** *Counter-signals.* For each tailwind, what
  would reverse it? Regulatory backlash, technical limit reached,
  market saturation, capital cycle turning.
- **(investigate)** *Things-to-monitor list.* Leading indicators
  we'd watch to reopen the timing call. Quantitative if possible.
- **(decide)** *Strategic-inflection-point check.* Is one force
  changing 10×? Per Grove — that's an inflection. The competitive
  landscape may reshape; existing analysis may be stale.

## Quick diagnostic

| Question | If no | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Is every trend backed by a quantitative signal, not just narrative? | Narrative-only | Find signal or downgrade to L confidence. |
| Are ≥3 trend categories scanned? | Monoculture | Add 2 more categories. |
| Does each trend name the implication for THIS opportunity? | Generic trend | Name implication + decision. |
| Is the timing call explicit (too-early / window / too-late / unclear)? | Silent on timing | Make the call; name drivers. |
| Are counter-signals listed? | Tailwind-only | List what would reverse the trend. |
| Is a things-to-monitor list present? | One-shot research | Add leading indicators to monitor. |
| Has the strategic-inflection-point check been run? | Steady-state assumed | Check for 10× force changes. |
