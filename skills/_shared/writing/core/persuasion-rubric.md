# Persuasion Rubric

The make-it-land method. Used by `persuade` (build it) and `diagnose` (score
the gap). Apply only where the stakes are genuine — read
[`narrative-honesty-guard.md`](./narrative-honesty-guard.md) before
manufacturing tension.

## The stickiness check (six dimensions)

For a talk, pitch, exec summary, or launch post, test the core message against:

- **Simple** — one core idea, ruthlessly prioritized. If the reader remembers
  one thing, what is it? Five big ideas means none lands.
- **Unexpected** — open a curiosity gap: a counterintuitive claim or a
  surprising number that the piece then resolves ("Huh?" → "Aha!").
- **Concrete** — specific people doing specific things, not abstractions and
  "synergies." Concrete detail gives every reader the same hook.
- **Credible** — let the reader verify rather than trust: a self-testable
  claim, one telling statistic, a vivid detail only an insider would know.
- **Emotional** — anchor stakes in one identifiable person or case. People act
  for individuals, not categories.
- **Story** — wrap the idea in a short challenge→action→result so the reader
  rehearses the move.

## The present↔future shape

Build the spine by oscillating between **what is** (current reality the reader
accepts) and **what could be** (the better future you're proposing); end on
the new state, not the status quo. Plant **one memorable peak** — a single
dramatized moment, image, or repeatable line people quote back. (Stagecraft —
props, timed reveals — is presentation-native; on the page, the peak is the
one line or figure that sticks.)

## Audience as hero

Frame the piece around the reader's aspiration and problem; position yourself
or your work as the **guide**, not the hero. Name the problem on three levels
where relevant — the external problem, the internal frustration it causes, and
why it matters — and lead with the felt one.

## Compression tools

- **ABT** — compress any pitch to one sentence: *[context] **and** [context],
  **but** [tension], **therefore** [action]*. The fastest way to find the
  spine of a sprawling doc. Too many "ands" = a flat info-dump; too many
  "buts" = a muddle.
- **Grunt test** — a stranger should be able to answer *what is this, why does
  it help me, what do I do next* within a few seconds of the opening.
