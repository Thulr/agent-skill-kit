# Talk & Pitch Playbook

## Scope

- **In:** anything whose job is to move an audience to feel or act — a
  presentation, a pitch, a launch post, an exec summary, a fundraising or
  project narrative.
- **Out:** a how-to (→ `technical-doc.md`), a neutral decision memo whose job is
  the analysis (→ `argument-memo.md`), a long-form essay (→ `narrative.md`).
- **Intents this surface answers:** `persuade`, `structure` (design); `revise`,
  `diagnose` (audit).

## Grounding

- *Made to Stick* (Chip & Dan Heath, 2007) — the six-part stickiness checklist
  (simple, unexpected, concrete, credible, emotional, story) and the expert
  blind spot.
- *Resonate* (Nancy Duarte, 2010) — oscillate between what-is and what-could-be;
  plant one memorable peak; cast the audience, not the speaker, as the hero.
- *Building a StoryBrand* (Donald Miller, 2017) — the audience is the hero and
  you are the guide; compress to a one-liner; pass the few-second stranger test.
- *The Narrative Gym / "ABT"* (Randy Olson, 2015) — the *and/but/therefore*
  spine.

> Stagecraft — props, timed reveals, live pacing — is presentation-native. On
> the page, port the *shape* (the present↔future turn, the one peak), not the
> theatrics.

## Good signals

- One core idea the audience could repeat afterward; everything serves it.
- An early curiosity gap — a surprising claim or number the piece then resolves.
- Concrete people and things, not abstractions and "synergies."
- The audience's aspiration is centered; the speaker/brand is the guide.
- It ends on the changed future state, not the status quo.
- One memorable line or image people will quote back.

## Common failures

- **Org-centric framing** — "here's what we built / what we did," instead of
  "here's what you can now do." The audience isn't the hero.
- **Five big ideas** — no prioritization, so nothing is remembered.
- **Abstraction** — the claim never touches a concrete person or number, so it
  doesn't stick and can't be verified.
- **No stakes** — nothing is gained or lost, so there's no reason to act.
- **Manufactured drama** — fake urgency or an invented villain; see
  `narrative-honesty-guard.md`. Costs trust.
- **Status-quo ending** — closes on "what is" instead of the better future.

## Heuristics

- **(persuade) Lead with one core** — force-rank to the single idea; cut
  everything that isn't it. If they remember one thing, name it.
- **(persuade) Open a curiosity gap** — a counterintuitive claim or surprising
  number up front, resolved later ("Huh?" → "Aha!").
- **(persuade, structure) Audience as hero** — write from the audience's
  aspiration; you are the guide who offers a path.
- **(persuade) What-is / what-could-be** — build the spine by contrasting
  present reality with the better future; end on the new state.
- **(persuade) One memorable peak** — plant a single line, image, or figure
  engineered to be repeated.
- **(persuade, revise) Make it concrete** — replace one abstraction per
  paragraph with a specific person doing a specific thing.
- **(diagnose) Stickiness scan** — score the core against the six dimensions
  (see `persuasion-rubric.md`); name which are missing.
- **(diagnose) Grunt test** — a stranger can state what this is, why it helps
  them, and what to do next within seconds.

## Quick diagnostic

| Question | If no | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Is there one core idea? | Nothing is remembered | Force-rank; cut to the core |
| Is the audience the hero? | Reads as bragging | Reframe to their aspiration |
| Is there a what-could-be? | No reason to move | Add the future-state contrast |
| Is the claim concrete + testable? | Doesn't stick | Add a person, a number, a detail |
| Is the tension real? | Reads as manipulation | Drop the manufactured drama |
