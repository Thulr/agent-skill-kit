# Conduct Playbook

Running the live conversation — how to behave in the room (or on the call) so the
person does the talking and the truth has space to surface.

## Scope

- In: opening and building rapport; managing silence; probing and following threads;
  staying neutral; closing by asking for a real commitment or next step.
- Out: choosing who to talk to (→ `prep`), pre-writing the questions (→
  `critique-questions`), and post-interview sensemaking (→ `synthesize`).
- Cross-references: if the session keeps drifting to opinions, the question set may need
  `critique-questions`.

## Grounding

- *Interviewing Users* (Portigal) — make it a conversation that grows, not an
  interrogation; embrace silence instead of supplying answers; move broad to specific
  and follow the thread; listen actively.
- *The Mom Test* (Fitzpatrick) — talk less than the participant; end with a concrete
  ask for commitment (time, reputation, or money) rather than "keep me posted."

## Good signals

- The participant is talking most of the time; you are mostly listening and probing.
- Silences are left open and the person fills them with the more considered answer.
- Questions open broad, then drill into the specific story the person raised.
- The session ends with a concrete next step or a commitment, not a vague good feeling.

## Common failures

- Marching through a fixed list, ignoring the interesting thread the person just opened.
- Talking too much — explaining, pitching, or narrating — and contaminating the signal.
- Rescuing every pause by offering multiple-choice answers, which hands the person your
  hypothesis to agree with.
- Nodding hardest at the answers you hoped for, steering the participant toward them.
- Accepting verbal enthusiasm ("I'd definitely use that") as a result and closing
  without asking for anything real.

## Heuristics

- Open with low-stakes, easy questions to build rapport before the harder ones; treat
  it as a conversation, not a script readout.
- After asking, stop talking. Let the silence sit; the reflective gap usually yields
  the richer answer.
- Go broad first, then follow the specific thread the person cares about with probes
  ("what happened next?", "why did that matter?").
- Stay neutral — keep your reactions even so you are not generating the signal you then
  "hear."
- Aim for the participant to hold most of the airtime; if you are talking more than a
  third of the time, pull back.
- Close by asking for a real commitment — time, reputation, or money — or a concrete
  next step; treat enthusiasm with no commitment as noise, not a yes.

## Quick diagnostic

- Who is talking more, you or them? You → ask, then go quiet.
- Did you fill the last silence with suggested answers? Yes → next time, wait.
- Are you reacting visibly to "good" answers? Yes → flatten your responses to stay neutral.
- Did the meeting end with only "sounds great"? Yes → you missed the ask; push for a
  commitment or next step.
