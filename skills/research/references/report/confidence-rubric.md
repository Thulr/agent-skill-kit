# Confidence rubric (H / M / L)

Every load-bearing claim in a research report is tagged H, M, or L.
Confidence reflects the strength of the *evidence behind the claim*,
not the certainty of the model generating the report.

## H — High confidence

The claim is supported by:
- **Multiple independent, well-triaged sources** (≥2 primary or
  institutional-secondary sources that don't share authors,
  institutions, or funding), **or**
- **A single peer-reviewed primary source with consensus replication**
  (other independent sources have replicated the finding), **or**
- **Documented standards / regulations / specifications** (the claim
  describes what the standard says, citing the standard text).

Promote to H only when contradicting evidence has been actively
searched for and not found.

## M — Medium confidence

The claim is supported by:
- **A single well-triaged source** with no known contradictions, **or**
- **Multiple sources of mixed quality** (one peer-reviewed + one or
  more editorial sources, all aligned), **or**
- **Industry consensus without primary measurement** (multiple
  industry sources agree but no peer-reviewed study exists).

M is the realistic ceiling for many claims about emerging topics
where primary research is sparse.

## L — Low confidence

The claim is supported by:
- **A single non-peer-reviewed source** (blog post, vendor doc,
  practitioner essay), **or**
- **Inferred synthesis across sources that don't directly support
  the claim** (the report combined two cited facts to derive a
  third — that derivation is the L claim), **or**
- **Contested evidence** (well-triaged sources disagree).

L claims must either:
- Be reframed as **explicit inferences** ("inferred from X+Y") in the
  report, or
- Be reframed as **open questions** in the Key debates section, or
- Be marked L and read as "this is suggestive, not established."

## What is "load-bearing"?

A claim is load-bearing if removing it changes the report's
conclusions or shifts the reader's takeaways. Background and
definitions usually aren't load-bearing. Current-state assertions,
debate positions, and implications are.

If you're unsure whether a claim is load-bearing: tag it anyway.
Over-citing is recoverable; under-citing isn't.

## Confidence ≠ importance

A high-confidence claim can be trivial. A low-confidence claim can
be the most important thing in the report. Don't conflate the two:
the confidence tag describes the *evidence*, not the *significance*.

## Updating confidence

Confidence is set at synthesis time based on what was found in the
search. If new sources surface later — for example, during a re-run
or a follow-up — update the tag rather than leaving it stale. A
report that doesn't re-evaluate its own confidence becomes less
trustworthy over time.
