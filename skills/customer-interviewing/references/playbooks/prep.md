# Prep Playbook

Planning a discovery interview *before* anyone talks — what you are trying to learn,
who you should hear it from, how you reach them, and how often.

## Scope

- In: naming a single learning goal tied to a real decision; defining and qualifying
  a target segment; writing a short screener; choosing a recruiting source; setting a
  sustainable cadence; deciding how many conversations are enough for now.
- Out: writing the question wording itself (→ `critique-questions`), running the
  session (→ `conduct`), making sense of results (→ `synthesize`), and quantitative
  surveys or market sizing (→ `research`).
- Cross-references: hand the drafted questions to `critique-questions` before fielding.

## Grounding

- *Continuous Discovery Habits* (Torres) — recruit a participant *pipeline* rather
  than re-recruiting per study; sustain a regular cadence; let a real decision, not
  curiosity, set the learning goal.
- *The Mom Test* (Fitzpatrick) — a session is only worth booking if it can change a
  decision; plan for behavior you can observe, not opinions you will collect.

## Good signals

- The plan states one learning goal as a decision it will inform ("if X, we will Y").
- The segment is specific enough that you could tell, from a screener, who is in or out.
- Recruiting draws on a repeatable source (in-product opt-in, advisory panel, sales/CS
  handoff), not just whoever is reachable this week.
- The cadence is a rate the team can actually hold (a few per week), not a one-off batch.

## Common failures

- Recruiting whoever is easiest — friends, fans, the loudest power users — and reading
  their agreeable answers as representative signal.
- No learning goal, so the conversation becomes a feature-request intake and every
  interview "succeeds" without changing anything.
- A screener that telegraphs the wanted answer, letting eager respondents qualify
  themselves in.
- Treating recruiting as a one-time sprint, so the practice dies after the first batch.

## Heuristics

- Write the learning goal as a decision first; if you can't name the decision it
  informs, you are not ready to recruit.
- Define the segment by *behavior* ("people who did X in the last month"), not by
  demographics or attitude.
- Keep the screener short and make the qualifying criteria hard to game — ask about
  recent actions, not intentions, and avoid hinting at the "right" profile.
- Build a standing source of participants so the next interview does not start from
  zero; automate the ask at a natural product moment when you can.
- Size the round to the decision: a handful of conversations to surface a pattern now,
  not a statistically representative sample — interviews are generative, not confirmatory.
- Set a cadence you can sustain weekly; consistency beats occasional large pushes.

## Quick diagnostic

- Can you name the decision this interview will inform? No → write the learning goal
  before recruiting.
- Could a screener cleanly separate in-segment from out-of-segment people? No → tighten
  the segment to an observable behavior.
- Do you have a repeatable way to reach the next five participants? No → set up a
  pipeline source before scaling the cadence.
- Are you recruiting from convenience? Yes → name the bias and add a source that reaches
  the real segment.
