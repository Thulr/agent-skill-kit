# Synthesize Playbook

Turning raw conversations into something the team can act on — one interview at a time,
then across interviews — without losing the evidence or smuggling in your hopes.

## Scope

- In: reducing a single interview to a one-page snapshot; building an experience map of
  the specific story; tagging takeaways with evidence; finding patterns across several
  interviews; separating behavior from claims.
- Out: planning and recruiting (→ `prep`), question wording (→ `critique-questions`),
  live facilitation (→ `conduct`), and authoring a polished narrative report (→
  `writing-design`).
- Cross-references: patterns that look decision-ready usually need an experiment before
  you bet on them — that is `product-discovery`/`research`, not synthesis.

## Grounding

- *Continuous Discovery Habits* (Torres) — capture each conversation as a one-page
  interview snapshot with an experience map of that specific story, so colleagues
  absorb the learning without reading raw notes; forwarding notes or recordings is an
  anti-pattern.
- *The Mom Test* (Fitzpatrick) — weigh committed actions and concrete past behavior
  over compliments and stated intentions when deciding what an interview proved.

## Good signals

- Each interview is reduced to a single page anyone can skim and reuse later.
- Takeaways point at specific things the person did or said, not paraphrased vibes.
- Behavioral evidence ("they did X") is kept distinct from claims ("they said they'd do X").
- At least one disconfirming signal is named where the data contains one.

## Common failures

- Confirmation bias — mining transcripts for the quotes that support the idea you
  already had, and dropping the rest.
- Dumping unsynthesized notes or a recording on the team and expecting them to do the
  synthesis you skipped.
- Collapsing many interviews into an average that hides the most informative outliers.
- Counting compliments and stated intentions as if they were observed behavior.

## Heuristics

- Reduce each interview to a one-page snapshot: who they are, the specific story
  (as an experience map), and a few evidence-tagged takeaways.
- Tag every takeaway with its basis — observed behavior, a direct claim, or your
  inference — and keep the three visibly separate.
- When patterning across interviews, group by what people *did*, then check whether what
  they *said* lines up; flag the gaps.
- Actively look for the interview that contradicts the emerging pattern and account for
  it rather than discarding it.
- Treat a committed action (gave time, made an intro, paid) as stronger evidence than
  any verbal enthusiasm.
- Stop short of "validated": say what the interviews make more or less likely, and name
  the experiment that would actually test the bet.

## Quick diagnostic

- Could a teammate act on this without reading the raw notes? No → write the snapshot.
- Are behavior and claims mixed together? Yes → separate them and re-weight.
- Did you keep only the supporting quotes? Yes → go find and account for the
  disconfirming ones.
- Are you about to call it "validated"? Yes → downgrade to a likelihood and name the
  test that would confirm it.
