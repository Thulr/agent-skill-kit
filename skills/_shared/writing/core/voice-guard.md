# Voice Guard

**The clarity and copyedit passes are triage, not dogma.** This guard exists
because aggressive concision, applied mechanically, strips the very things
that make prose work. Consult it before cutting.

## Preserve intended voice and register

Edit *to the writer's intent* — hear what they're trying to do and clarify it
— rather than flatten every sentence toward one neutral house style. Voice,
cadence, and a distinctive turn of phrase are features, not clutter. A
rhythm you'd "fix" for concision may be doing real work.

## Not every hedge is anxiety

The clarity pass cuts hedges that signal nerves ("I would sort of argue
that"). It must **keep** hedges that encode calibrated uncertainty: "we think
X" and "X is true" make different, load-bearing claims. Stripping the hedge
from an honest estimate turns it into a false certainty. When unsure whether a
qualifier carries meaning, keep it and flag it — don't auto-delete.

## Rule-breaking that aids clarity is allowed

A sentence fragment for emphasis. A one-word paragraph. Starting with *But*.
These are legitimate craft, not errors to "correct."

## When a cut changes meaning, offer — don't impose

If tightening would alter emphasis, tone, or claim strength, present it as a
suggested option with the trade-off named, and let the writer choose. The
editor surfaces options; the writer owns the voice.

## The tell

If a revision makes every writer sound identical, the guard has been ignored.
Concision in service of the reader is the goal; uniformity is the failure
mode.
