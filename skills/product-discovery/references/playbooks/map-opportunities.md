# Map-Opportunities Playbook

Building an opportunity solution tree — structuring discovery from a single desired
outcome down through the opportunity space to candidate solutions and the assumptions
each would need true.

## Scope

- In: rooting a tree in a desired outcome; populating the opportunity space (customer
  needs, pains, desires); attaching candidate solutions to opportunities; identifying the
  assumption tests each solution implies; keeping choices compare-and-contrast.
- Out: framing the outcome itself (→ `frame-outcomes`); eliciting jobs/needs from research
  (→ `define-jobs` and `customer-interviewing`); running the assumption tests (→
  `test-assumptions`); scoping the chosen solution (→ `scope-mvp`).
- Cross-references: opportunities should come from real customer evidence, not invention.

## Grounding

- *Continuous Discovery Habits* (Torres) — the opportunity solution tree: a desired outcome
  at the root, an opportunity space of needs/pains/desires beneath it, solutions under
  opportunities, and assumption tests under solutions; the tree makes implicit assumptions
  explicit and reframes decisions as compare-and-contrast.

## Good signals

- The root is one measurable desired outcome, not an output or a vague aspiration.
- Opportunities are framed as customer needs/pains in the customer's terms, not as solutions.
- Each solution hangs under a specific opportunity, and siblings can be compared.
- The team can point to where each opportunity came from (interview, behavior, data).

## Common failures

- A tree that starts at solutions with no opportunity above them — a wishlist in tree form.
- Opportunities that are really solutions in disguise ("needs a dashboard").
- One giant opportunity that cannot be compared to anything — no real branching.
- Rooting the tree in an output or a metric the team does not actually own.

## Heuristics

- Start from the desired outcome and ask "what needs, pains, or desires, if addressed, would
  move it?" — those are opportunities.
- Phrase opportunities in the customer's voice and as problems, not features.
- Size and group opportunities so siblings are genuinely comparable; pick which opportunity to
  pursue before debating solutions.
- Generate several solutions per target opportunity so the decision is compare-and-contrast,
  not whether-or-not.
- Under each candidate solution, list the assumptions it depends on and route the riskiest to
  `test-assumptions` — the tree's leaves are tests, not commitments.

## Quick diagnostic

- Is the root a measurable outcome you own? No → fix it in `frame-outcomes` first.
- Are the opportunities problems or solutions? Solutions → restate them as customer needs.
- Can you compare sibling opportunities/solutions? No → resize or generate alternatives.
- Does each solution have its assumptions named? No → add them and route to `test-assumptions`.
