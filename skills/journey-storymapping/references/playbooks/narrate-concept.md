# Narrate-Concept Playbook

Crafting the concept or origin story — a short, structured narrative that aligns a team or
pitches the experience vision around the user's transformation.

## Scope

- In: writing the concept story (what the product makes possible) or the origin story (how a user
  discovers and arrives); structuring it on a compact arc; anchoring it in the protagonist's want
  and change.
- Out: mapping the full usage journey (→ `map-journey`); diagnosing a live experience (→
  `diagnose-experience`); polishing the prose, deck, or talk itself (→ `writing-design`).
- Cross-references: the concept story usually distills the climax/payoff from a `map-journey` arc.

## Grounding

- *The User's Journey* (Lichaw) — the concept and origin story types; using a narrative arc to
  ideate, align stakeholders, and pitch an experience around the user-as-protagonist.

## Good signals

- The story centers the user-protagonist's want and the change they experience, not a feature list.
- It has stakes and a turn — a before and an after — so it lands emotionally.
- It is short enough to retell; a team can repeat it after hearing it once.
- It distinguishes concept (the value/why) from origin (the arrival/onboarding) clearly.

## Common failures

- A feature tour dressed up as a story — capabilities narrated with no protagonist or stakes.
- No transformation — nothing changes for the user, so there is nothing to root for.
- Conflating the concept story with the full usage journey; the pitch should distill, not enumerate.
- Hero-product framing — the company/product is the protagonist instead of the user.

## Heuristics

- Open on the protagonist's status quo and the tension/want; close on the changed state the product
  enables — that gap is the story.
- Keep it to the essential beats (setup → turn → payoff); a concept story is a distillation, not a
  full map.
- Choose the type deliberately: concept story to sell the why, origin story to show how someone
  arrives and commits.
- Make it repeatable — if the team can't retell it, tighten it.
- Hand off prose/slide/talk polishing to `writing-design`; this playbook owns the structure, not the copy.

## Quick diagnostic

- Is the user the protagonist? No → recast away from the product-as-hero.
- Is there a clear before-and-after change? No → add the transformation.
- Concept or origin — is the type deliberate? No → pick one.
- Could a teammate retell it after one hearing? No → distill it further.
